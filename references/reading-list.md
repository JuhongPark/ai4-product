## Science Fiction (Mapped to Research Questions)

### "What happens when you remove humans from AI product development?"

**Ted Chiang — *The Lifecycle of Software Objects* (2010)**
- 150 pages. The only novel about AI product lifecycle.
- Platform dependency, organizational collapse, long-term AI "care" as human labor.
- Matches: MIT 95% failure, Klarna reversal, AADM "AI Absent" zone.
- English difficulty: ★★★☆☆ (clear prose, software terminology familiar to PMs)

### "What value do humans retain when AI can do everything?"

**Kazuo Ishiguro — *Klara and the Sun* (2021)**
- Nobel Prize winner. AI narrator with intentionally simple English.
- AI is perfect at devotion but cannot assign meaning; human value lies in imperfect relationships.
- Matches: Nature meta-analysis (creation > decision), Selective Complementarity.
- English difficulty: ★★☆☆☆ (deliberately simple sentences)

### "What does full automation do to society?"

**Kurt Vonnegut — *Player Piano* (1952)**
- Machines replace all labor → management elite vs. purposeless masses.
- Predicted Brynjolfsson's "Turing Trap" 70 years early.
- Matches: Devin AI displacing junior programmers, organizational change research.
- English difficulty: ★★★☆☆ (short sentences, 1950s American English, ironic tone)

### "What does human-AI collaboration actually look like?"

**Martha Wells — *All Systems Red* (Murderbot Diaries #1, 2017)**
- 144 pages. AI narrates in first person; humorous, direct prose.
- AI constantly negotiates autonomy boundaries with human team.
- Matches: Selective Complementarity, human-AI trust dynamics.
- English difficulty: ★★☆☆☆ (colloquial, short, funny)

### "How do platforms and corporations wield AI power?"

**Cory Doctorow — *Red Team Blues* (2023)**
- Tech corporate power, surveillance capitalism, infrastructure control.
- Matches: Stanford HAI (AI compute concentration), Partnership on AI (sovereignty).
- English difficulty: ★★★☆☆

### "What happens when technology promises more than it delivers?"

**Neal Stephenson — *Snow Crash* (1992)**
- Metaverse origin story; brilliant surface vs. dystopian reality underneath.
- Matches: agent washing (Gartner), 95% pilot failure.
- English difficulty: ★★★★☆ (dense, information-heavy)

**Philip K. Dick — *Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?* (1968)**
- When AI becomes indistinguishable from human, what is "human"?
- Matches: anticipatory ethics, empathy as last human differentiator.
- English difficulty: ★★★☆☆

### "What if autonomous AI pursues its own goals?"

**William Gibson — *Neuromancer* (1984)**
- Self-directed AI (Wintermute) as archetype for agentic AI.
- Matches: AI 2027 scenario, alignment crisis.
- English difficulty: ★★★★★ (dense cyberpunk prose; challenging even for native speakers)

## Suggested Reading Order

### For Research Context
```
1. Mollick — Co-Intelligence (AI collaboration now)
2. Brynjolfsson — The Turing Trap (augmentation > replacement)
3. Cooper — AI Transformation of Product Innovation (NPD+AI)
4. Dunne & Raby — Speculative Everything (futures methodology)
```

### For Fiction (easiest first)
```
1. Wells — All Systems Red (144pp, fun, fast)
2. Ishiguro — Klara and the Sun (beautiful, simple English)
3. Chiang — The Lifecycle of Software Objects (our topic exactly)
4. Vonnegut — Player Piano (automation's endgame)
```

### For English Learning (by difficulty)
```
★★☆ Klara and the Sun — intentionally simple, Nobel-quality prose
★★☆ All Systems Red — colloquial, humorous, very short
★★★ Lifecycle of Software Objects — clear technical narrative
★★★ Player Piano — American irony training
★★★ Do Androids Dream — noir atmosphere
★★★ Red Team Blues — contemporary tech thriller
★★★★ Snow Crash — dense but rewarding
★★★★★ Neuromancer — peak difficulty
```
