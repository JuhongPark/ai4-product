# Organizational Resistance to AI Adoption in Product Development

> Evidence-based analysis of why organizations struggle to adopt AI, who resists most,
> and what strategies actually work to overcome resistance (2023--2026).

---

## 1. The Scale of the Problem

Organizational resistance to AI adoption is not a fringe concern --- it is the dominant
barrier to realizing value from AI investments. The data paints a consistent picture:
technology is rarely the bottleneck; people and processes are.

### The 70/20/10 Rule

BCG's October 2024 report *Where's the Value in AI?* found that **70% of AI
implementation challenges are people- and process-related**, 20% are technology problems,
and only 10% involve AI algorithms themselves. Companies that follow the "10-20-70" rule
--- allocating 10% of resources to algorithms, 20% to technology/data, and 70% to people
and process change --- consistently outperform those that over-index on technology.

- Source: [BCG, "Where's the Value in AI?" (Oct 2024)](https://media-publications.bcg.com/BCG-Wheres-the-Value-in-AI.pdf)

### CEO-Level Acknowledgment of Resistance

- **45% of CEOs** say most of their employees are resistant or even openly hostile to AI
  (HR Dive / Asana survey, 2024).
- **7 in 10 leaders** say their workforce is not ready to leverage AI tools successfully.
- **95%** of senior executives have invested in AI, but only **14%** have aligned
  workforce, technology, and growth goals (EY, 2025).
- **64% of managers** say employees fear AI tools will make them less valuable at work.

Sources:
- [HR Dive: Nearly half of CEOs say employees are resistant or hostile to AI](https://www.hrdive.com/news/employers-employees-resistant-hostile-to-AI/749730/)
- [EY survey on AI productivity gaps (Nov 2025)](https://www.ey.com/en_gl/newsroom/2025/11/ey-survey-reveals-companies-are-missing-out-on-up-to-40-percent-of-ai-productivity-gains-due-to-gaps-in-talent-strategy)

### The Trust Collapse of Mid-2025

Deloitte's TrustID Index revealed that **trust in company-provided generative AI fell 31%
between May and July 2025**. Even more strikingly, trust in **agentic AI** --- systems
that can act independently rather than just recommend --- **dropped 89%** during the same
period. Usage of official tools declined, and nearly half of frontline employees migrated
to unapproved alternatives.

- Source: [People Managing People: Trust Crisis --- The Cultural Fault Lines Behind America's AI Anxiety](https://peoplemanagingpeople.com/workforce-management/ai-trust-gap/)

### The Skills Gap as the Primary Barrier

Deloitte's 2025 survey of 3,235 leaders found that the **AI skills gap is the single
biggest barrier** to integration. Education --- not role redesign or workflow changes ---
was the number-one way companies adjusted their talent strategies in response to AI. A
2024 Deloitte survey of executives found **39% cited lack of internal talent** as the
biggest AI adoption barrier.

- Source: [Deloitte: State of AI in the Enterprise 2026](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/what-we-do/capabilities/applied-artificial-intelligence/content/state-of-ai-in-the-enterprise.html)
- Source: [Deloitte: AI adoption in the workforce](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/topics/emerging-technologies/ai-adoption-in-the-workforce.html)

### The Training Deficit

BCG's 2025 *AI at Work* report surveyed 10,635 employees across 11 nations and found:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Regular AI users (leaders, managers, frontline) | 72% |
| Regular users who received **zero** training | 18% |
| Employees who feel training is sufficient | 36% |
| Users with 5+ hours of training who became regular users | 79% |
| Users with <5 hours of training who became regular users | 67% |

The gap between 79% and 67% is significant: **5+ hours of structured training is a
threshold** that measurably shifts adoption outcomes.

- Source: [BCG: AI at Work 2025](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/ai-at-work-momentum-builds-but-gaps-remain)

### The Pilot-to-Scale Chasm

McKinsey's 2025 *State of AI* report found that nearly two-thirds of firms remain in the
**experimentation (32%) or piloting (30%)** stages. Only **31% report scaling AI
enterprise-wide**, and just **39% see enterprise-level EBIT impact**. The transition from
pilot to production --- what many call "pilot purgatory" --- remains the defining
challenge.

- Source: [McKinsey: The state of AI in 2025](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai)

## 2. Taxonomy of Resistance

Organizational resistance to AI is not monolithic. Different individuals and groups resist
for different reasons, and effective intervention requires diagnosing the specific type of
resistance at play.

### 2.1 Fear-Based Resistance: "AI Will Replace Me"

**Prevalence:** 59% of global employees fear job displacement due to automation (Edelman
Trust Barometer, 2025). In organizations undergoing comprehensive AI-driven redesign,
**46% of employees** worry about job security, compared to 34% in less advanced companies
(BCG, 2025).

**Manifestations:**
- Active avoidance of AI tools to avoid demonstrating that tasks can be automated.
- Hoarding knowledge and refusing to document processes that could be codified.
- Framing AI skepticism as quality concerns when the real driver is existential anxiety.

**Evidence:** Newer research (2025--2026) has identified a broader syndrome called
**"algorithmic anxiety"** --- encompassing not just fear of job loss but deeper concerns
about human value, professional identity, and the meaning of work in an automated future.
This manifests as the **"Fear of Becoming Obsolete" (FOBO)**: the creeping sense that
skills are degrading in real time and the window to stay relevant is closing.

Sources:
- [Edelman Trust Barometer 2025: AI Trust Imperative](https://www.edelman.com/trust/2025/trust-barometer/report-tech-sector)
- [People Managing People: Employee AI Fears in 2026](https://peoplemanagingpeople.com/workforce-management/ai-fears-2026/)

### 2.2 Competence-Based Resistance: "I Don't Know How to Use This"

**Prevalence:** 18% of regular AI users received no training at all (BCG, 2025). Only 36%
of employees feel their training is adequate.

**Manifestations:**
- Low-quality prompting that produces poor AI output, confirming skepticism.
- Requesting training repeatedly but not acting on it (learned helplessness).
- Delegating AI usage to junior team members rather than building personal competence.

**The Dunning-Kruger effect in AI adoption:** Research shows that individuals with the
lowest levels of AI experience are slightly more likely to be algorithm-averse. Those with
moderate knowledge tend toward automation bias (over-trusting AI), and those with the
highest knowledge levels achieve appropriate calibration.

Sources:
- [BCG: AI at Work 2025](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/ai-at-work-momentum-builds-but-gaps-remain)
- [Tandfonline: (Over)Trusting AI Recommendations](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10447318.2023.2301250)

### 2.3 Workflow-Based Resistance: "This Doesn't Fit How I Work"

**Prevalence:** The most insidious form of resistance because it often looks like
acceptance. Employees sign up for AI tools, attend training, and report positive
sentiments --- but never integrate AI into their actual workflows.

**Manifestations:**
- Using AI tools for trivial tasks (summarizing emails) while core workflows remain
  unchanged.
- Reverting to pre-AI processes under time pressure ("I can do it faster myself").
- Workarounds that bolt AI onto existing processes rather than redesigning them.

**Evidence:** BCG's 2025 research found that workflow redesign --- not just tool
deployment --- is what separates AI leaders from laggards. Companies that redesigned
workflows around AI capabilities reported significantly higher value realization than
those that simply added AI to existing processes.

### 2.4 Trust-Based Resistance: "I Don't Trust the Output"

**Prevalence:** In the Microsoft *Dear Diary* study, developers' views on the
trustworthiness of AI-generated code **remained unchanged** even after weeks of positive
usage experiences. The Harvard/BCG "jagged frontier" study demonstrated that consultants
using AI were **19 percentage points less likely** to produce correct solutions on tasks
outside AI's capability boundary --- validating that some trust concerns are warranted.

**Manifestations:**
- Manually reviewing 100% of AI output, negating productivity gains.
- Refusing to share AI-assisted work with clients or stakeholders.
- Citing hallucination anecdotes as justification for blanket rejection.

Sources:
- [Microsoft Research: Dear Diary (Oct 2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.18334)
- [Harvard/BCG: Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=64700)

### 2.5 Identity-Based Resistance: "AI Threatens Who I Am"

The concept of **"AI identity threat"** captures resistance rooted not in rational
assessment of AI's capabilities but in perceived threat to one's professional identity.
Research identifies three central predictors: changes to the nature of work, perceived
loss of status or position, and the degree to which AI is perceived as encroaching on
identity-defining tasks.

The Microsoft *Dear Diary* study revealed that while developers found AI tools useful and
enjoyable, the experience did not reduce identity-related anxieties about what it means to
be a software engineer when AI can write code.

A 2024 study in *AI & Society* (Springer) developed a framework for **mitigating
AI-induced professional identity threat**, emphasizing the role of Explainable AI and
strong professional identity as protective factors.

Sources:
- [Springer: AI identity threat at the workplace](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12525-021-00496-x)
- [AI & Society: Mitigating AI-induced professional identity threat](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00146-024-02170-0)

### 2.6 Political Resistance: AI Shifts Power Dynamics

AI adoption reconfigures organizational power. When AI tools give junior employees access
to capabilities that previously required years of experience, or when data-driven
recommendations bypass middle management judgment, political resistance follows.

**Manifestations:**
- Middle managers blocking AI tool rollouts that expose their decision-making to scrutiny.
- Senior leaders resisting dashboards that make performance metrics more transparent.
- Turf wars between IT/data teams (who control AI infrastructure) and business units (who
  own the workflows).
- "Shadow AI" --- where employees adopt unapproved tools (78--86% of employees, depending
  on study) because official channels are controlled by resistant gatekeepers.

Source: [ScienceDirect: Navigating power dynamics through AI-driven algorithmic decision-making](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0740624X25000474)

### 2.7 Ethical Resistance: Concerns About Bias, Fairness, and Responsible Use

Not all ethical resistance is irrational. Employees may resist AI adoption out of
legitimate concerns about:

- **Bias in AI outputs:** Amazon ceased using an AI hiring algorithm in 2018 after it was
  found to discriminate against women applying for technical roles.
- **Privacy and surveillance:** AI-driven monitoring tools that track productivity,
  keystrokes, or emotions generate well-founded pushback.
- **Accountability gaps:** When AI makes a consequential error, it is unclear who bears
  responsibility --- the user, the team, or the vendor.
- **Regulatory risk:** The EU AI Act (effective August 2024) classifies AI systems by risk
  level, creating real compliance concerns for high-risk applications.

Sources:
- [Nucamp: Ethics of AI in the Workplace 2025](https://www.nucamp.co/blog/ai-essentials-for-work-2025-the-ethics-of-ai-in-the-workplace-risks-and-responsibilities-in-2025)
- [Springer: Ethical AI in the workplace --- fairness and transparency](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s43681-025-00903-5)

### By Seniority Level

A striking finding from recent research: **AI is seniority-biased in its impact.**
Following AI adoption, junior employment declines sharply relative to non-adopters, while
senior employment remains largely unchanged. The decline is concentrated in occupations
most exposed to generative AI and is driven by **slower hiring** rather than increased
separations.

- **Junior employees** are more likely to adopt AI tools (having grown up with them) but
  also most threatened by them. As one study noted, "the learning curve is being
  automated."
- **Senior employees** resist more often but face less immediate displacement risk. Their
  resistance is frequently rooted in identity and political concerns rather than fear of
  job loss.
- **Middle managers** face a particular threat, as AI-powered analytics can bypass their
  traditional role as information brokers between frontline teams and executives.
- **Two-thirds of business leaders** now say they would not hire someone without AI skills,
  and **71% would prefer a less-experienced candidate with AI skills** over a more
  experienced candidate without them.

## 4. The Trust Problem in Detail

### Multidimensional Trust Framework

Trust in AI is not a single variable. Research has identified multiple dimensions that
must be addressed independently:

| Trust Dimension | Definition | Example Failure Mode |
|----------------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Cognitive Trust** | Rational assessment of reliability, accuracy, transparency | "The model hallucinates too often" |
| **Emotional Trust** | Affective comfort and sense of safety | "I feel uneasy letting AI make this decision" |
| **Technical Trust** | Confidence in the system's engineering quality | "The system crashes; the UI is confusing" |
| **Social Trust** | Trust in the organization deploying AI and its intentions | "My company is using AI to replace us, not help us" |

A 2026 study in the *Journal of Management Studies* (Vuori) identified **four distinct
trust configurations** among organizational members:

1. **Full trust** (high cognitive + high emotional) --- leads to enthusiastic, productive
   adoption.
2. **Full distrust** (low cognitive + low emotional) --- outright rejection.
3. **Uncomfortable trust** (high cognitive + low emotional) --- users acknowledge AI works
   but feel uneasy; compliance without commitment.
4. **Blind trust** (low cognitive + high emotional) --- uncritical acceptance; high risk of
   automation complacency.

Neither cognitive nor emotional trust alone predicts adoption. Effective AI rollouts must
address both dimensions.

Sources:
- [ScienceDirect: Emotional and cognitive trust in AI](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S2352250X24000460)
- [Wiley: Unveiling the Combined Effect of Emotional and Cognitive Trust (2026)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/joms.13177)

### The Jagged Frontier: When Trust Should Be Calibrated, Not Maximized

The Harvard/BCG experiment with 758 BCG consultants (2023) demonstrated the danger of both
under-trust and over-trust:

- **Inside the AI capability boundary:** Consultants using GPT-4 completed **12.2% more
  tasks**, worked **25.1% faster**, and produced results of **40%+ higher quality**.
- **Outside the AI capability boundary:** Consultants using AI were **19 percentage points
  less likely** to produce correct solutions.

The key insight: **AI's capabilities form a "jagged technological frontier"** --- some
seemingly hard tasks are easy for AI, while some seemingly easy tasks are beyond it. The
boundary is non-obvious, creating a calibration challenge for users.

Source: [Harvard Business School: Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=64700)

### Over-Trust (Automation Complacency) vs. Under-Trust (Algorithm Aversion)

These are not personality traits; they are context-dependent behaviors:

**Automation complacency** emerges when users fall into "a state of passivity, diffidence,
or deference" toward technology they perceive as more proficient than themselves. Risk
factors include:
- High system reliability (creates complacency through consistency).
- Low task engagement (routine work where users stop paying attention).
- Low AI literacy (unable to detect when AI errs).

**Algorithm aversion** increases when:
- Stakes are high ("I can't risk trusting this for a medical diagnosis").
- The task feels subjectively personal or creative.
- Users have witnessed or heard about a single AI failure (the "once bitten" effect).
- Users have very low AI experience (Dunning-Kruger territory).

Recent research (2024) found these phenomena interact: the same user can exhibit
automation complacency on one task and algorithm aversion on another, depending on
perceived stakes and domain familiarity.

Sources:
- [Frontiers: Trust process for effective human-AI interaction](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/computer-science/articles/10.3389/fcomp.2025.1662185/full)
- [Oxford Academic: Bending the Automation Bias Curve](https://academic.oup.com/isq/article/68/2/sqae020/7638566)

### How Trust Evolves Over Time

Research traces a temporal arc:

1. **Formation phase:** Initial trust is largely driven by reputation, framing, and first
   impressions. The Edelman Trust Barometer (2025) found that hands-on experience is the
   fastest route to trust --- people who use AI more trust it more.
2. **Calibration phase:** Through repeated use, users develop a mental model of where AI
   is reliable and where it is not. This is the critical "jagged frontier" learning period.
3. **Stabilization phase:** Trust settles into a pattern, but remains dynamic. Feature-
   specific trust emerges: users may trust AI for data analysis but not for customer-facing
   communication.

### What Breaks Trust and How Hard It Is to Rebuild

Trust in AI is **asymmetric**: it takes many positive experiences to build and a single
failure to destroy. Key trust-breaking events:

- **Hallucination incidents** experienced personally (not just heard about).
- **Lack of transparency** about how AI outputs were generated.
- **Organizational betrayal:** when AI is deployed to monitor or evaluate employees without
  disclosure.
- **Performance degradation:** when AI models are updated and previously reliable outputs
  become unreliable.

McKinsey reports that **91% of organizations are unprepared to scale AI responsibly**, and
Accenture found that while **95% of employees valued working with generative AI**, they
do not trust their leaders to implement it thoughtfully.

Sources:
- [Edelman: When Familiarity Breeds Trust](https://www.edelman.com/insights/familiarity-breeds-trust-in-ai)
- [Nature: Trust in AI --- progress, challenges, and future directions](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41599-024-04044-8)

## 5. Proven Strategies for Overcoming Resistance

### 5.1 JPMorgan: Opt-In + Social Proof + Consumer-Grade UX

**Result:** 0 to 200,000--250,000 users in 8 months.

JPMorgan's LLM Suite rollout (summer 2024) is the gold standard for enterprise AI
adoption. Key design decisions:

- **Opt-in, not mandate:** No employee was forced to use LLM Suite. This created "healthy
  competition" as early adopters shared wins.
- **Social proof channels:** Internal social channels highlighted use cases and
  productivity gains, creating FOMO that accelerated sign-ups.
- **Consumer-grade UX:** The tool was designed to feel familiar, like using ChatGPT, not
  like navigating enterprise software.
- **Personal relationship with AI:** Derek Waldron (JPMorgan) described the goal as each
  employee developing "a personal relationship with AI."

The viral, opt-in approach bypassed political resistance by making adoption a bottom-up
cultural movement rather than a top-down mandate.

Sources:
- [AI CERTs: Inside JPMorgan AI adoption --- How LLM Suite Went Viral](https://www.aicerts.ai/news/inside-jpmorgan-ai-adoption-how-llm-suite-went-viral/)
- [McKinsey: JPMorgan Chase's Derek Waldron on building an AI-first bank culture](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/financial-services/our-insights/jpmorgan-chases-derek-waldron-on-building-an-ai-first-bank-culture)

### 5.2 Singtel: 95% Workforce Training at Scale

**Result:** 13,000 employees (95% of Singapore workforce) completed foundational AI
training.

Singtel's approach used a **tiered model:**
- **100% AI-ready:** All employees receive foundational training in generative AI, data
  analytics, and responsible AI use.
- **25% AI practitioners:** Deeper training for employees who will build and deploy AI
  solutions.
- **Union partnership:** Renewed MoU with the Union of Telecoms Employees of Singapore
  (UTES) to align workforce transformation with labor protections.

The union partnership is critical: it addressed fear-based resistance by demonstrating that
AI training was about enhancement, not replacement.

Sources:
- [Singtel: Group accelerates workforce transformation](https://www.singtel.com/about-us/media-centre/news-releases/singtel-group-accelerates-workforce-transformation)
- [HRM Asia: Singtel renews workforce training pact](https://hrmasia.com/singtel-renews-workforce-training-pact-to-strengthen-ai-skills-across-organisation/)

### 5.3 Mondelez: Explicit "AI Does Not Replace the Developer" Messaging

Mondelez's AI product development (AIPD) tool works 2--5x faster than traditional methods
and has led to 70 new product SKUs. The tool determines how formulas impact sensory
attributes, pricing, and nutrition, then finds optimal solutions.

Key to adoption: the tool was positioned as **augmenting** human developers, not replacing
them. The AI identifies the search space; the human developer makes the final creative and
strategic decisions. This framing directly addressed identity-based resistance among R&D
professionals.

Sources:
- [Confectionery News: How Mondelez is using AI to maximise product innovation](https://www.confectionerynews.com/Article/2025/03/19/stategies-confectioners-are-using-ai-to-maximise-product-innovation/)
- [Klover.ai: Mondelez AI Strategy Analysis](https://www.klover.ai/mondelez-ai-strategy-analysis-of-dominance-in-consumer-packaged-goods-confectionery-snacks/)

### 5.4 Salesforce: Dogfooding Before Selling

Salesforce used its **vast workforce as a proving ground** for Agentforce before selling
it to customers. This "dogfooding" approach served multiple purposes:

- **Built internal conviction:** Employees could attest to the tool's value from personal
  experience.
- **Identified failure modes** before they reached customers.
- **Created authentic case studies** for sales teams.

However, adoption challenges persisted even internally: only 8,000+ companies use
Agentforce externally despite 150,000+ signed-up users, demonstrating that even validated
tools face friction at scale.

Source: [Startup Hub AI: Salesforce Agentic AI --- Inside Its Internal Productivity Push](https://www.startuphub.ai/ai-news/ai-research/2025/salesforce-agentic-ai-inside-its-internal-productivity-push/)

### 5.5 P&G: Data Infrastructure First, Then AI Tools

P&G's approach inverted the typical AI rollout:

1. **Years of data infrastructure work:** Integrating ERP, manufacturing, and supply chain
   data across 100+ global sites before deploying AI.
2. **The AI Factory:** A platform that provides data scientists with automation and ML
   tools, making them "10x faster and more efficient."
3. **Build, not buy:** P&G built core AI capabilities internally rather than relying on
   third-party tools, ensuring deep integration with existing workflows.

This infrastructure-first approach addressed workflow-based resistance: by the time AI
tools arrived, the data and processes were ready to support them.

Sources:
- [MIT Sloan Review: How Procter & Gamble Uses AI](https://sloanreview.mit.edu/article/how-procter-gamble-uses-ai-to-unlock-new-insights-from-data/)
- [Klover.ai: P&G's AI Strategy](https://www.klover.ai/procter-gamble-ai-strategy-path-to-ai-dominance-consumer-goods/)

### 5.6 BCG: The 5-Hour Training Threshold

BCG's own data provides a clear prescription: **5+ hours of structured training shifts
adoption from 67% to 79%**. The training must include:

- **Instruction** (what AI can and cannot do).
- **In-person sessions** (hands-on practice with real workflows).
- **Coaching** (ongoing support for calibrating trust and identifying use cases).

Without this investment, organizations are leaving 12 percentage points of adoption on the
table.

### 5.7 Progressive Autonomy: Advise -> Assist -> Automate

The most effective adoption strategies use a graduated model:

| Stage | AI Role | Human Role | Trust Level Required |
|-------|---------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Advise** | Provides suggestions and recommendations | Reviews every output before action | Low --- user maintains full control |
| **Assist** | Automates routine tasks | Approves higher-risk actions | Medium --- user trusts routine decisions |
| **Automate** | Handles predictable workflows independently | Focuses on strategy and oversight | High --- user trusts autonomous operation |

This progression allows teams to build trust incrementally while maintaining operational
control. Organizations that jump straight to "Automate" trigger resistance;
those that start with "Advise" and prove value before escalating see smoother adoption.

Sources:
- [Microsoft Learn: AI Agent Adoption Guidance](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/cloud-adoption-framework/ai-agents/)
- [PTC: How AI Agents Are Accelerating Digital Transformation](https://www.ptc.com/en/blogs/corporate/ai-agents-accelerate-digital-transformation)

## 6. When Resistance Is Actually Correct

Not all resistance to AI adoption is irrational or counterproductive. Some of the most
important product decisions in recent years came from employees who pushed back against
AI initiatives.

### 6.1 Microsoft Recall: Resistance as Security Signal

In May 2024, Microsoft announced Recall as a flagship capability for Copilot+ PCs. The
feature continuously captured screenshots of user activity and stored them in a searchable
database.

**What resisters flagged:**
- Security researcher Kevin Beaumont demonstrated that "stealing everything you've ever
  typed or viewed on your own Windows PC is now possible with two lines of code."
- The original implementation stored screenshots in an **unencrypted SQLite database**
  accessible to any application.
- Signal president Meredith Whittaker called it "a dangerous honeypot for hackers."

**What happened:** Microsoft postponed the launch (originally June 2024, eventually
delayed to December), overhauled the architecture to encrypt the database, and made Recall
**opt-in by default** instead of opt-out.

**Lesson:** The resistance came from security researchers and privacy advocates --- people
whose professional expertise gave them legitimate standing to question the feature.
Dismissing their concerns would have been a catastrophic product decision.

Sources:

- [Malwarebytes: Microsoft Recall delayed after privacy and security concerns](https://www.malwarebytes.com/blog/news/2024/06/microsoft-recall-delayed-after-privacy-and-security-concerns)

### 6.2 Google AI Overviews: Speed Over Quality

In May 2024, Google launched AI Overviews for all U.S. users. The feature quickly
generated embarrassing errors:
- Suggesting users **eat rocks** or **apply glue on pizza**.
- Providing dangerously incorrect advice about rattlesnake bites.
- Claiming to have children and sharing "their favorite recipe."

Google temporarily restricted the feature and made adjustments. Industry observers noted
the rush to launch was driven by competitive pressure from OpenAI, not by the feature
being ready.

**Lesson:** When internal quality concerns are overridden by competitive urgency, the
"resistant" employees are often the ones with the best product judgment. The damage to
Google's search credibility was significant.

### 6.3 The Difference Between Resistance to Change and Resistance to Bad Ideas

A useful heuristic for distinguishing the two:

| Signal | Resistance to Change | Resistance to Bad Ideas |
|--------|---------------------|------------------------|
| **Who resists** | Broad, diffuse resistance across roles | Specific resistance from domain experts |
| **What they cite** | Vague concerns ("I don't like it") | Specific technical or ethical issues |
| **When they resist** | Before and during training | After understanding the system |
| **How they propose alternatives** | They don't; they just avoid | They offer specific improvements |
| **Track record** | They resisted the last change too | They adopted previous tools willingly |

Organizations should create **structured channels for dissent** --- ways for employees to
raise concerns about AI implementations that are taken seriously rather than dismissed as
"resistance to change." The cost of ignoring legitimate pushback (as in the Recall and AI
Overviews cases) far exceeds the cost of investigating it.

## 7. Measuring and Monitoring Resistance

### Beyond Usage Rates: A Multi-Layer Measurement Framework

Usage rates alone are dangerously misleading. A recent study (2025) found that AI coding
assistants delivered a **20% increase in PRs per author** but **incidents per pull request
jumped 23.5%** and **change failure rates increased 30%**. High usage without quality
measurement creates an illusion of successful adoption.

| Layer | Metric | What It Reveals |
|-------|--------|----------------|
| **Access** | % with tool access | Deployment breadth (necessary but insufficient) |
| **Activation** | % who used tool at least once | Initial curiosity / compliance |
| **Regular Use** | % using weekly or daily | Habit formation |
| **Workflow Integration** | # of workflows with embedded AI | Depth of adoption beyond experimentation |
| **Quality Impact** | Task quality, error rates, rework | Whether AI use improves outcomes |
| **Sentiment** | Employee satisfaction, trust scores | Leading indicator of sustainability |
| **Skill Development** | Self-assessed and tested competence | Training effectiveness |

Source: [Cortex: A Framework for Measuring Effective AI Adoption in Engineering](https://www.cortex.io/post/a-framework-for-measuring-effective-ai-adoption-in-engineering)

### The Microsoft Dear Diary Insight: Mixed Methods Over Telemetry

The *Dear Diary* study's greatest methodological contribution was demonstrating that
**telemetry alone is insufficient**. The study combined:

- **Surveys:** Baseline and follow-up measurements of attitudes, trust, and perceived
  usefulness.
- **Randomized controlled trial:** Treatment and control groups to isolate causal effects.
- **Three-week diary study:** Daily entries capturing qualitative experience, workflow
  changes, and emotional responses.

Key finding: the diary entries revealed **unexpected uses of AI** (replacing web search,
creative ideation) that would never have appeared in usage telemetry. They also captured
trust concerns and identity anxieties that surveys alone missed.

Source: [Microsoft Research: Dear Diary (Oct 2024)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.18334)

### Qualitative vs. Quantitative Methods

| Method | Strengths | Limitations |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| **Usage telemetry** | Objective, scalable, real-time | Misses quality, sentiment, workarounds |
| **Surveys** | Captures attitudes and satisfaction at scale | Self-report bias; snapshot in time |
| **Diary studies** | Rich context; captures workflow changes over time | Labor-intensive; small samples |
| **Interviews** | Deep exploration of resistance drivers | Not scalable; interviewer bias |
| **Manager observation** | Context-rich; captures team dynamics | Subjective; managers may miss shadow AI |
| **A/B experiments** | Causal attribution | Ethical and practical constraints |

**Best practice:** Use **quantitative methods for breadth** (how many people are adopting,
at what rate) and **qualitative methods for depth** (why some resist, what barriers they
face, how workflows actually change).

### Leading Indicators of Adoption Failure

Based on cross-referencing the research, the following are **early warning signs** that an
AI rollout is heading toward failure:

1. **Manager non-adoption:** If managers are not using AI tools, their teams are unlikely
   to follow. Manager usage is the single strongest leading indicator.
2. **Training satisfaction below 40%:** When fewer than 40% of employees feel training is
   sufficient, adoption stalls at the competence barrier.
3. **Shadow AI prevalence above 50%:** When more than half of employees use unapproved AI
   tools, it signals that official tools are failing to meet needs --- a workflow and trust
   problem.
4. **Rising incident rates alongside rising usage:** The coding assistant study's finding
   (20% more PRs, 23.5% more incidents) is a red flag that AI is being used without
   appropriate calibration.
5. **Sentiment decline after initial enthusiasm:** A post-honeymoon drop in satisfaction
   that is not addressed quickly can calcify into permanent resistance.
6. **No workflow redesign:** If AI tools are bolted onto existing processes without any
   process change, value realization will plateau and adoption will regress.

## Summary: Key Takeaways

1. **The problem is people, not technology.** 70% of AI implementation challenges are
   people- and process-related. Organizations that allocate resources accordingly (the
   10-20-70 rule) are the ones that scale successfully.

2. **Resistance is heterogeneous.** Fear, competence, workflow, trust, identity, political,
   and ethical resistance require different interventions. A single "change management"
   program will not address all seven.

3. **Training has a threshold effect.** 5+ hours of structured, multi-modal training
   (instruction + practice + coaching) shifts regular usage from 67% to 79%. Below that
   threshold, organizations are leaving adoption on the table.

4. **Trust is multidimensional and asymmetric.** Cognitive and emotional trust must both be
   addressed. Trust takes many positive experiences to build and a single failure to
   destroy.

5. **The Global South leads adoption.** India (92%) and the Middle East (87%) outpace
   Europe and North America, driven partly by fewer regulatory constraints and partly by
   urgency.

6. **Opt-in beats mandate.** JPMorgan's viral, social-proof-driven rollout of LLM Suite
   (0 to 250K users in 8 months) outperformed top-down approaches that trigger political
   resistance.

7. **Some resistance is signal, not noise.** Microsoft Recall and Google AI Overviews
   demonstrate that employee and expert pushback can be the most valuable product feedback
   an organization receives. Build channels for dissent.

8. **Measure beyond usage.** Telemetry alone is insufficient. The best organizations
   combine quantitative usage data with qualitative methods (diary studies, interviews) to
   understand the full picture of adoption, resistance, and impact.
